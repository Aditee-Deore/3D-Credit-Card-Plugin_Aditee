# Niyo — Photography Guidelines

## 01 Core Philosophy

> Every photograph we make must feel like a memory worth keeping.

Niyo photography is not documentation. It is emotional storytelling rooted in real travel, real people, and the real feeling of moving through the world with freedom and clarity.

Our images do not sell a product. They sell the feeling of being the kind of person who travels well — prepared, confident, and open to what is next.

### The Niyo Image Must Always
- Feel earned — as if the photographer was genuinely present in the moment, not staging it
- Carry emotional temperature — warmth, excitement, calm, or wonder — never neutrality
- Serve a brand pillar — wanderlust, belonging, clarity, confidence, or discovery
- Work without text — the image must carry meaning before any copy is added
- Be technically precise — sharp where sharpness matters, soft where softness serves emotion

---

## 02 Brand Emotional Pillars

Every Niyo photograph must consciously serve one of these five emotional pillars. Before shooting, identify which pillar the image is meant to activate.

### Wanderlust
*The feeling of wanting to explore something you have never seen.*
**Visual expression:** Open vistas, leading lines, figures moving toward the horizon, golden-hour silhouettes, motion blur on moving subjects.

### Belonging
*The warmth of being accepted and at home in a foreign place.*
**Visual expression:** Candid connections, shared meals, eye contact, hands exchanging things, local markets, children playing.

### Clarity
*The relief of understanding something complex becoming simple.*
**Visual expression:** Clean product shots, uncluttered environments, natural directional light, deliberate negative space.

### Confidence
*The quiet assurance of someone who knows they are prepared.*
**Visual expression:** Strong posture, decisive gestures, direct eye contact with the camera, bold urban environments.

### Discovery
*The electric surprise of encountering something new and unexpected.*
**Visual expression:** Micro details, textures, unexpected angles, reflections, layered environments, peek-through compositions.

## 03 What to Avoid

These are active disqualifiers. An image with any of these qualities should not be used in Niyo communications, regardless of technical quality.

| Avoid | Why |
|---|---|
| Stock-photo smiles | Posed expressions that look performed rather than felt. |
| Symmetrical staging | Environments arranged so perfectly they feel artificial. |
| Lifestyle clichés | Laptop on beach, coffee with a view, phone at the Eiffel Tower. |
| Flat colour | Images with no tonal variation or emotional temperature. |
| Unclear subject | Images where the eye does not know where to land within two seconds. |
| Text in frame | Signage, overlays, or brand language baked into the photograph. |
| Watermarks or borders | Any external attribution that breaks the native feel. |
| Flash-heavy lighting | Flat artificial light that erases depth and shadow. |
| Over-saturation | HDR-style colour that feels digital and processed. |
| Tourist-trap locations | Any location that reads as a postcard rather than an experience. |

## 04 Global Visual Identity

Niyo photography must feel Indian in origin and global in aspiration. Our travellers carry Indian culture with them. The camera must honour both where they come from and where they are going.

### Indian in Origin
- Skin tones from across the South Asian spectrum, always rendered with warmth and dignity
- Cultural markers — textiles, food, gesture, expression — treated as subjects of beauty, not exoticism
- Authentic Indian environments: urban, rural, coastal, and everywhere between

### Global in Aspiration
- International environments photographed with the same care as domestic ones
- Subjects who belong in the world — not tourists passing through it
- Light and colour that works across geographies without losing Niyo's warmth signature

### Diversity Is Non-Negotiable
Every shoot must include visible diversity in age, gender expression, skin tone, ability, and body type. This is not a quota. It is an accurate representation of who Niyo serves and aspires to serve.

## 06 Global Grading Signature

The Niyo grading signature is warm, rich, and humanistic. It borrows from documentary film — slightly lifted shadows, controlled highlights, and a skin-tone warmth that works across geographies.

### Contrast
- Contrast is present but never harsh. Aim for a cinematic S-curve, not a lifted curve.
- Blacks should be rich but not crushed — retain shadow detail in the lowest two stops.
- Whites should be bright but not clipped — a small amount of highlight rolloff is always preferable to blown highlights.

### Highlights
- Pull highlights down by 20–35% in post as a starting point.
- Golden-hour images: let the specular highlights on skin and metal glow, but control sky highlights.
- Indoor images: highlight rolloff prevents the clinical look of overlit spaces.

### Shadows
- Lift shadows by 10–20 points. Never shoot for maximum black — the Niyo signature is open and accessible.
- Warm the shadow tones: add red and remove blue in the shadow HSL curve. This is the single most important step for South Asian skin tone rendering.
- Outdoor shadows should feel like shade, not night.

### Saturation
- Global saturation: small positive adjustment (5–10 points). Never desaturate.
- Targeted: increase blues (+15), reds (+10), yellows (+12). Reduce greens slightly to avoid synthetic appearance.
- Skin tones: protect with HSL orange channel — reduce saturation only if the subject is overexposed, never as a general rule.

## 07 White Balance System

White balance is not used for accuracy in Niyo photography. It is used to control emotional temperature. These are starting targets — adjust for subject and scene.

| Lighting condition | Target Kelvin | Tint | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| Daylight / blue sky | 5200–5600 K | Neutral to slight green | Clean, confident, editorial. |
| Golden hour | 5800–6500 K | Slight magenta | Warm, romantic, aspirational. |
| Overcast | 5800–6200 K | Neutral | Moody, documentary. |
| Indoor tungsten | 3200–3600 K | Slight green | Warm, intimate, interior travel. |
| Indoor LED / mixed | 4000–4800 K | Neutral | Commercial and product contexts. |
| Night / neon | 3500–4500 K | Varies by scene | Vibrant, cinematic, urban. |

> Cooler than 4000 K in a warm scene reads as clinical. Warmer than 6800 K reads as old and yellowed. Stay within range.

## 09 Camera Rendering Philosophy

Niyo photography prioritises dynamic range over in-camera contrast. Always shoot with the flattest available picture profile when shooting to edit, and the richest available film simulation when shooting to camera JPEG.

### Shooting Mode
- RAW always preferred over JPEG for any image destined for editorial or campaign use.
- JPEG with Fujifilm film simulation is acceptable for social and real-time content.
- Video: Log format wherever available. F-Log, S-Log3, or C-Log3 as camera allows.

### Exposure
- Expose to the right: protect highlights, recover shadows in post.
- Metering: evaluative or matrix for complex scenes. Centre-weighted for portraits.
- Never clip skin. Highlights on background can be sacrificed; skin cannot.

## 10 Lens System

Lens choice is a storytelling decision. Each focal length produces a distinct relationship between subject and environment. Use it intentionally.

| Focal length | Character | Primary use |
|---|---|---|
| 16–24 mm | Ultra-wide | Establishing environment shots. Architecture, airports, landscapes. Always keep horizon level. |
| 28–35 mm | Documentary | Street scenes, markets, candid travel moments. Primary storytelling focal length for Niyo. |
| 50 mm | Intimate | Portraits, product-in-hand, connection moments. True-to-life perspective. |
| 90–105 mm | Macro | Currency, texture, micro-detail, product close-ups. Use only for deliberate detail emphasis. |

### Lens Principles
- Prime lenses preferred over zoom for portrait and detail work — the discipline of fixed focal length improves composition.
- Fast aperture (f/1.4–f/2.8) for portraits and low-light narrative.
- Stop down to f/5.6–f/11 for landscape and environment.
- Never use digital zoom. Move your feet.

## 11 ISO System

Grain is not failure. Used intentionally, grain is warmth, authenticity, and film character. The Niyo ISO system embraces it at every level.

| ISO range | Context | Look |
|---|---|---|
| ISO 100–400 | Daylight and golden hour | Clean, rich, full colour fidelity. |
| ISO 400–800 | Overcast and indoor | Slight warmth. Acceptable grain that adds texture without distraction. |
| ISO 800–1600 | Dusk, evening, low light | Visible but intentional grain. Shadow detail preserved. |
| ISO 1600–3200 | Night and indoor markets | Cinematic grain acceptable. Prioritise mood over technical perfection. |
| ISO 3200+ | Extreme low light only | Use grain as a deliberate aesthetic. Never apologise for it — lean in. |

## 12 Shutter Speed System

| Speed | Primary use |
|---|---|
| 1/2000 s+ | Freeze motion. Sport, splashing water, vehicles. |
| 1/500–1/1000 s | Walking pace, hand-held candid portraits. |
| 1/60–1/250 s | Standard shooting. Most editorial and product. |
| 1/15–1/60 s | Intentional motion blur on background. Panning shots. |
| < 1/15 s | Long exposure. Light trails, silky water. Tripod required. |

### Motion Blur Philosophy
Motion blur is permitted and encouraged in contexts where it communicates movement, energy, or the passage of time. A panning shot of a tuk-tuk with a sharp subject and blurred background is more emotionally honest than a frozen frame. Deliberate blur is a stylistic choice — accidental blur is not.

---

## 13 Focus Philosophy

### Eyes Are Always Sharp
In any image with a human subject, the nearest eye to the camera must be sharp. This is non-negotiable. Eyes are where the viewer connects with the subject, and soft eyes communicate technical failure, not artistic intent.

### Selective Focus
- Use depth of field to guide the eye — not to hide a weak composition.
- f/1.4–f/2 for intimate portrait moments. The subject must be completely sharp.
- f/2.8–f/4 for subject-in-environment. Sharp subject, slightly soft background.
- f/5.6–f/11 for landscape and architecture. Everything in focus.

### Focus Mode
- Continuous AF with subject tracking for all moving subjects.
- Single AF with manual focus confirmation for product and macro.
- Eye-detection AF always on for human portraits.

---

## 14 Sharpness Philosophy

Niyo photography is sharp where sharpness creates connection and soft where softness creates emotion. Sharpness is not a measure of quality — it is a compositional tool.

- Global sharpening in post: light hand (10–20 points). Over-sharpening introduces digital artefacts.
- Selective sharpening: eyes, product edges, currency details, and text in frame.
- Never sharpen skin — it reveals texture that should remain soft.
- Micro-contrast (clarity / texture): low for portraits, moderate for landscape, high for detail and texture shots.

## 15 Lighting Philosophy

> Natural light is always the first choice. Artificial light is an apology.

### Natural Light Hierarchy

| Light quality | Character | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Golden hour | Warm, directional, long shadows | Portraits, travel establishing shots, product on location. |
| Open shade | Soft, neutral, diffused | Candid portraits, market scenes, close-ups. |
| Overcast | Even, moody, documentary | Street photography, architecture, reportage. |
| Directional window | Soft, indoor, natural wrap | Interior lifestyle, product, intimate portraits. |
| Blue hour | Cool, atmospheric, ambient | Cityscapes, architecture, environmental portraits. |

### Artificial Light Rules
- Flash: only bounce flash or large modifier (octabox, beauty dish). Never bare on-camera flash.
- Continuous LED: tungsten-balanced for warmth. Blue-toned LED panels are not permitted.
- Reflectors: gold or silver for outdoor fill — never white reflectors, which read as clinical.
- Never overlight. A slightly underlit authentic moment beats a technically perfect artificial one.

---

## 16 Composition Philosophy

Niyo composition is purposeful and restrained. We do not fill frames — we organise them. Every element in frame must earn its place.

### Principles
- Rule of thirds as a starting point, not a rule. Break it with intention.
- Leading lines toward the subject: roads, corridors, rivers, shadows.
- Negative space is active space — it gives the eye room to breathe and the subject room to exist.
- Layers: foreground, midground, background. Three layers create depth. Two layers create flatness.
- Horizon: always intentional. Level unless tilt serves a specific emotional purpose.
- Frame within frame: doorways, windows, arches, overgrowth as compositional containers.

### Avoid
- Centred symmetrical compositions — they read as corporate and static
- Cluttered frames with no clear subject
- Cropping at joints — knees, wrists, ankles
- Dead-on eye-level with subjects — slightly above or below creates dynamism

## 17 Human Direction

Niyo subjects are people in the middle of living — not models performing travel. Direction must produce authenticity, not performance.

### Direction Principles
- Give subjects an action, not a pose. "Walk toward the market" not "stand here and look left."
- Capture the moment before and after the directed moment — the transition is often the best frame.
- For candid shots: photograph without direction first. Only redirect when the environment is not cooperating.
- Eye contact with camera: use deliberately. Not every image needs it — turned away or mid-action often reads more authentic.
- Genuine laughter: never ask for it. Create the condition for it — conversation, a story, a joke between takes.
- Hands: always give subjects something to do with their hands. Empty hands produce stiff posture.

### Expression Hierarchy

| Priority | Type | Description |
|---|---|---|
| First | In-the-moment | Subject is genuinely absorbed in their activity. |
| Second | Reacting | Subject has just noticed or responded to something real. |
| Third | Directed candid | Subject is given a real action; expression is natural response. |
| Last resort | Posed | Subject is explicitly positioned. Use only for product-forward shots. |

---

## 18 Styling Philosophy

Niyo styling is life-authentic, not editorial-perfect. Clothes should look worn, not selected. Environments should look lived-in, not set-dressed.

### Wardrobe
- Colour-aware: garments should harmonise with the colour science system, not fight it.
- Indian textiles are celebrated — sarees, kurtas, and block-print garments are appropriate in cultural contexts.
- International travel wardrobe: practical, personal, and specific to the character. Not aspirational lifestyle-brand attire.
- No logos, branded graphics, or identifiable third-party marks.
- Avoid pure white or pure black garments unless deliberately lit and stylised.

### Environment
- Props must be functional — items the subject would genuinely carry or use.
- Niyo card may appear naturally in frame — never force-placed or spotlit.
- Backgrounds: real locations preferred over sets. A real airport gate beats a constructed one.
- Remove anachronistic or brand-inconsistent elements from frame before shooting.

---

## 19 Product Integration Rules

When the Niyo card or app appears in photography, its treatment defines brand quality. Product integration must feel like discovery, not advertising.

- Card: always held naturally — never flat on a surface unless it is a deliberate macro shot.
- Screen: app interface must be legible and show a relevant state — not a blank or generic screen.
- Neither card nor phone should compete with the human subject for visual dominance.
- Lighting on the card: specular highlights on the card surface are acceptable and look premium. Avoid direct flash reflection.
- Card angle: 15–30 degrees from horizontal reads best. Perfectly flat reads as a product pack-shot, not lifestyle.
- Integration naturalness: if removing the card from the image would not change the emotional reading, the integration is probably forced — reconsider.

---

## 20 Texture Philosophy

Texture is what makes an image feel tactile and real. Niyo photography prioritises physical texture as a storytelling device — it is what separates our imagery from AI-generated or stock photography.

- Prioritise surfaces with inherent texture: woven fabrics, stone, wood, water, skin.
- Sidelight to reveal texture — light from 30–60 degrees to the surface will reveal it. Frontal light flattens it.
- Grain as texture: film grain in post adds micro-texture to digital images and brings them into the Niyo visual register.
- Avoid smooth, plastic, or synthetic textures as the primary subject — they communicate the opposite of authenticity.
- In food and market photography: moisture, dust, and imperfection are to be preserved, not corrected.

## 22 Environmental Direction

The environments we photograph signal where Niyo belongs in the world. Choose them with the same intentionality as casting.

### Preferred Environments

| Environment | Why |
|---|---|
| Airports and transit | Terminals, departure boards, aircraft windows, passport control. Conveys readiness and movement. |
| Local markets | Indian bazaars, street food stalls, international markets. Conveys cultural immersion and belonging. |
| Urban environments | City streets, cafes, public transport, hotel lobbies. Conveys cosmopolitan confidence. |
| Natural landscapes | Mountains, coastlines, forests. Conveys wonder and the scale of travel. |
| Domestic interiors | Kitchens, living rooms, study spaces. Conveys the preparation and planning side of travel. |
| International quarters | Foreign neighbourhoods, local cafes abroad, international campuses. Conveys belonging away from home. |

### Avoid
- Empty, generic office environments — they communicate nothing about travel or financial freedom
- Generic "luxury" locations: yachts, private jets, penthouse pools — they misrepresent who Niyo serves
- Locations that require model-release complexity without adding significant emotional value
- Any environment where the location is more famous than the story it contains

## 23 Final Rendering Signature

Before any Niyo photograph is approved for use, it must pass this rendering check. Apply it in order.

| Step | Check | Question |
|---|---|---|
| 01 | Emotional pillar | Which of the five pillars does this image activate? If none, do not use. |
| 02 | Subject clarity | Does the eye land on the subject within two seconds? If not, recompose or reject. |
| 03 | Skin tone | Are South Asian skin tones warm, detailed, and dignified? If not, re-grade. |
| 04 | Colour signature | Does the overall palette feel warm and aligned with the Niyo system? If not, re-grade. |
| 05 | Grain and texture | Does the image feel tactile and real — or smooth and synthetic? Add grain if digital flatness is present. |
| 06 | Avoid list | Does the image contain anything on the avoid list? If yes, reject or correct. |
| 07 | Text independence | Does the image carry emotional meaning without any text? If not, it is not a Niyo photograph. |

> A Niyo photograph is not the best-looking image from the shoot. It is the most honest image that also serves the brand.
